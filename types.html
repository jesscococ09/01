Telemedicine

2 Types

Synchronous Telehealth

Synchronous telehealth happens when patients and healthcare providers communicate at the same time through tools like phone calls or video chats. This type of telehealth recreates the feel of an in‑person visit, allowing both sides to talk, ask questions, and interact directly. Its purpose is to provide real‑time care that can lead to a diagnosis, treatment plan, or prescription when needed. These sessions may include typical interview questions, but they can also involve simple physical tasks, such as standing, walking, or moving limbs so the provider can observe balance or mobility. Providers may also check things like hygiene by asking the patient to show their hands or other details on camera while discussing daily routines and living conditions.

Asynchronous Telehealth

Asynchronous telehealth involves communication that doesn’t happen at the same moment. Instead of both sides interacting live, information is shared in pieces over time. This method, often called “store and forward,” lets healthcare workers collect details and send them to another provider to review later. For example, a staff member might record an interview with a patient in a rural clinic and then pass that recording to a specialist, who examines it and creates a diagnosis or treatment plan when they receive it.

Remote Monitoring

Remote patient monitoring, or RPM, is a type of telehealth that works without live communication between the patient and provider. Instead, it uses digital devices to gather health information and send it to a clinician, who reviews the data later to track conditions, spot potential issues, and offer guidance. Most of this information is collected at home through tools like weight‑tracking scales, heart‑rate monitors, blood‑sugar meters, blood‑pressure cuffs, sleep‑monitoring devices, breathing trackers, and fetal monitoring equipment used during pregnancy.

Telepharmacy

Telepharmacy uses communication technologies to provide pharmacy services from a distance, giving people access to medication support even when they can’t physically reach a pharmacy because of travel limits, mobility challenges, or schedule conflicts. Through tools like video calls, mobile apps, secure messaging systems, and digital health records, pharmacists can check prescriptions, offer medication guidance, and review a patient’s history to ensure treatments are safe and appropriate. Newer technologies, including artificial intelligence and machine learning, help flag possible drug interactions or adherence problems so issues can be addressed early. By combining digital platforms with professional expertise, telepharmacy broadens access to medication management and strengthens patient safety across many different care settings

Telerehabilitation

Telerehabilitation uses communication technology to provide rehabilitation services without requiring in‑person contact. Because rehab often depends on observing movement and functional abilities, delivering it remotely calls for methods that let providers watch, listen, and assess from a distance. Tools such as two‑way video platforms, audio‑only calls, remote patient‑monitoring devices, and store‑and‑forward systems make this possible by transmitting images, data, or educational materials for later review. People typically access telerehabilitation through devices like smartphones, tablets, computers, or telephones, and the approach must account for the fact that not everyone has the same technology available. Many individuals choose this option because it can speed up communication, support quicker evaluations, improve access to specialists when travel is difficult, reduce costs, offer more flexible scheduling, and limit exposure to infections.
